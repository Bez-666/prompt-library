# The Tattoo Artist's Guide to Working in Asia
### By Richard Beston | Updated April 2026

---

I've been tattooing professionally for over two decades. For the last several years, I've been based in Thailand, working between studios in Bangkok and the south, while still taking regular bookings in the UK, Germany, and Sweden. I judge at conventions across Asia and Europe, and I've built a strong client base in India too. Asia is home now — and I wouldn't change it.

But when I first started travelling for work out here, nobody told me *anything*. Not the visa stuff, not the customs stuff, not the unwritten rules about what you can and can't tattoo in certain countries. I learned it all the hard way — through awkward conversations at immigration desks, through expensive mistakes, and through the generosity of local artists who set me straight.

This guide is everything I wish someone had handed me before my first flight out. It covers six countries where I've either worked, judged, or have close contacts on the ground: Thailand, Japan, Bali, Singapore, Hong Kong, and South Korea. For each one, I've laid out the visa and work permit situation honestly — including the grey areas — plus customs tips, conventions, studio areas, daily costs, cultural rules, and how to actually land a guest spot.

Use it. Share it. And if something's changed since I last updated it, drop me a message.

**— Richard**

## 🇹🇭 Thailand

Thailand is where I live, so I'll start here. It's the most popular destination in Asia for guest-spotting tattoo artists, and for good reason — the cost of living is low, the tattoo scene is massive, and the clients are international. But don't mistake "relaxed vibe" for "no rules."

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

Thailand requires a **Non-Immigrant B Visa** plus a **Work Permit** for any foreign national doing paid work — and yes, that includes tattooing.

- **Non-Immigrant B Visa:** Apply before arrival at a Thai embassy in your home country. Cost: ~$80–200 USD (฿2,800–7,000). Initial validity: 90 days.
- **Work Permit:** Your Thai host studio must apply on your behalf through the Ministry of Labour's Office of Foreign Workers Administration. Cost: ~$90–300 USD (฿3,150–10,500). Processing: 7–14 working days. The employer must maintain a minimum ratio of 4 Thai employees per foreign worker.
- **TM30 Registration:** Mandatory within 24 hours of arrival. Usually handled by your accommodation or employer.
- **Overstay penalties:** ฿500/day, up to ฿20,000.

**The guest-spot grey area:** Here's the reality. Tattooing is *not* on Thailand's list of reserved occupations (unlike carpentry or certain handicrafts), which means it's not explicitly banned for foreigners. But enforcement of work permit requirements for short-stay guest artists varies wildly. Doing an unpermitted paid guest spot carries a fine of up to **฿50,000 (~$1,400 USD)** and possible deportation. Many artists operate informally on tourist visas. I'm not recommending it — I'm telling you what happens. The safest path is having the host studio sponsor your Non-B visa and work permit for the duration of the guest spot.

**Convention note:** Artists attending conventions as competitors or visitors on a tourist visa occupy a grey zone. If you're actively tattooing paying clients at a convention booth, you technically need a work permit.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

No specific prohibition on importing personal tattoo equipment into Thailand.

- **Machines:** Carry in your cabin bag. Lithium batteries (wireless machines) *must* travel in carry-on — universal airline rule.
- **Needles:** No import ban, but commercial-looking quantities may attract scrutiny. Keep them in original sterile packaging.
- **Inks:** Standard airline liquid rules for cabin (≤100ml per bottle, clear bag). Larger bottles in checked luggage. No known import ban on tattoo inks.
- **Pro tip:** Keep everything in organized, clearly labelled professional cases. Carry a convention invitation or studio invitation letter. If customs asks, you're attending a convention or visiting a studio — have the paperwork to back it up.

### 🎨 Cultural Sensitivities

This is the section that matters most in Thailand. Get it wrong and you'll have real problems.

- **Buddha imagery:** The Thai government considers Buddha face tattoos disrespectful. Tattooing or displaying them can attract fines up to ฿500 and serious social disapproval. **Never** place Buddha imagery below the waist on a client — that's considered deeply offensive.
- **Royal family imagery:** Protected under strict lèse-majesté laws (Article 112). Any perceived disrespect carries **severe criminal penalties** — we're talking prison. Never tattoo royal imagery. Full stop.
- **Sacred Thai script (Yantra/Sak Yant):** Tattooing sacred text without understanding the meaning, or tattooing it incorrectly, is culturally offensive. If a client requests this, consult a Thai practitioner.
- **Acceptable imagery:** Elephants (national symbol), lotus flowers, Thai-style geometric patterns, abstract designs — all fine.
- **Temples:** Visitors with visible tattoos are generally welcome unless the tattoo depicts explicit or sacrilegious content. Cover provocative tattoos as a courtesy.
- **Studio etiquette:** Remove shoes when entering studios that observe this practice.

- **Instagram** is the dominant platform. DM studios directly with your portfolio. Most Bangkok studios are Instagram-active. Search hashtags: #bangkoktattoo, #thaistudio, #bkktattoo.
- **iNKPPL** has a directory of Thai artists and studios: [inkppl.com](https://inkppl.com/en/tattooevents/thailand)
- **Conventions:** Apply as a booth artist at Thailand Tattoo Expo, Irezumi War, or Pattaya events. Booth fees are typically paid by the artist.
- **Email pitches:** Many higher-end Bangkok studios list contact emails on their websites. Send a clean portfolio PDF, your preferred dates, your style, and any prior guest spot references.
- **Timing:** Rainy season (June–October) is quieter for tourism but can mean lower client bookings. High season (November–February) brings more demand.
- **Facebook groups:** Thailand tattoo community groups are active and helpful for connections.

## 🇯🇵 Japan

Japan is a dream destination for tattoo artists, but it comes with complexity. The culture, the history, the art — it's all incredible. The bureaucracy and social attitudes around tattoos? That's the challenge.

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

The big news first: Japan's **2020 Supreme Court ruling** confirmed that tattooing is an artistic act, not a medical procedure, making it legal for non-doctors to tattoo. That was a landmark decision that changed everything for the domestic industry.

But for foreign artists wanting to *work* in Japan, the visa situation is still complicated:

- **Artist Visa (Geijutsu):** For foreign nationals pursuing creative activities. Requires a substantial portfolio, proven career, work plan in Japan, and a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) obtained in Japan before travel. COE processing: 1–3 months. Full timeline: potentially 3–6 months. Important: tattoo artistry doesn't fit cleanly into traditional "fine arts" categories, so acceptance varies.
- **Working Holiday Visa:** Available to citizens of ~30 countries (Australia, NZ, Canada, UK, etc.). In theory WHV holders can work, but immigration classifies tattooing as potentially falling under "public morals" restrictions. Grey area.
- **Employer-sponsored Work Visa:** Very few Japanese tattoo studios are set up to sponsor a foreign artist's work visa.
- **Tourist / visa-exempt entry:** Most Western nationalities can enter visa-free for 90 days. Doing paid tattooing on tourist entry is technically illegal. But enforcement at the individual artist level is very low, and many international guest spots happen informally.

**The honest reality for guest spots:** There is effectively no clean, fast legal pathway for a short-term tattoo guest spot in Japan. Most international artists working guest spots do so on tourist entries, which is technically non-compliant. Carry studio invitation letters and a professional portfolio as supporting documentation if questioned. Each artist needs to assess this risk for themselves.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

Tattoo equipment is not explicitly prohibited from import into Japan, but discretion is your friend here.

- **Needles:** No specific import ban. Carry in original sterile packaging, organized and clearly labelled. Use plain unlabelled cases — skip the tattoo branding on your bags.
- **Inks:** Standard airline liquid rules for cabin. No known Japanese import restriction. Keep to working quantities.
- **Machines:** Carry in cabin (fragile, plus lithium battery rules). Have documentation available.
- **Key advice:** Customs staff may recognize tattoo equipment. In East Asia, discretion is recommended. Organize your kit so inspection is easy and non-alarming. Carry a studio invitation letter or convention confirmation.

### 💡 Practical Tips

**SIM cards:**
- Buy at Narita/Haneda airport or convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson). Tourist SIM: ~¥2,000–3,500 for 15–30 days of data (~$14 USD).
- Alternatively, purchase an eSIM in advance (AloSIM, Airalo).

**Getting around:**
- Get a **Suica or Pasmo IC card** immediately. Load it at any station machine. Works on all trains, buses, and even convenience stores.
- City metro: ¥150–350 per ride. Budget ¥800–1,500/day for urban travel.
- **Shinkansen:** Tokyo to Osaka one way ~¥14,000 (~$93). For extensive travel, a Japan Rail Pass may be worth it (7-day pass: ~¥50,000).
- Taxis are expensive (base fare ~¥500–600). Save them for late nights or heavy equipment transport.

**Daily budget for a working artist:**
- Budget: ¥12,000–18,000/day (~$80–120) — hostel, convenience store food, transit.
- Mid-range: ¥25,000–40,000/day (~$170–270) — private room, restaurant meals.
- Japan is mid-to-high cost. Budget carefully.

**Power:** Japan uses 100V, Type A plugs. Most modern devices handle 100–240V, but verify. Bring adapters for European equipment.

### 🎨 Cultural Sensitivities

This is absolutely critical in Japan. The tattoo-yakuza association is real and affects your daily life:

- **Tattoos and yakuza:** Japanese culture historically associates tattoos with organized crime, especially among older generations. This is the root of most tattoo restrictions you'll encounter.
- **Onsen (hot springs):** Most traditional onsen maintain a **strict no-tattoo policy**. This is a venue policy, not law. Your options:
  - Look for **tattoo-friendly onsen** — a growing list at [tattoo-friendly.jp](https://tattoo-friendly.jp).
  - Notable tattoo-friendly destinations: **Kinosaki Onsen** (Hyogo — all 7 public baths allow tattoos), **Beppu Onsen** (100+ tattoo-friendly facilities), **Hakone Yuryo** (private rooms).
  - Many onsen offer **private family baths** (kashikiri) where tattoos are quietly accommodated.
  - Waterproof tattoo cover patches are sold at convenience stores.
- **Gyms and pools:** Many major chains (Konami, Gold's Gym Japan) prohibit visible tattoos. Long sleeves usually work.
- **Public spaces:** No legal prohibition. Younger Japanese are significantly more accepting. Urban areas are far more relaxed than rural ones.
- **For artists:** Post-2020 Supreme Court ruling, tattooing is legal. No medical licence needed. But opening a studio requires health department registration with the local prefecture.

### 🤝 Finding Guest Spots

- **Instagram** is the primary channel. DM studios with your portfolio. Many studios that host international guests are English-literate.
- **Facebook** groups like "Tattoo Guest Spots in Japan" are active.
- **Tokyo Bay Tattoo Festival:** Applying as an attending artist is a direct in-road to the Japanese tattoo community.
- **Network at conventions abroad:** Japanese artists attend international conventions (Singapore Ink Show, etc.). Meeting them in person before pitching a Japan guest spot is highly effective.
- **Key nuance:** Guest spots in Japan are often arranged informally. Studios may frame it as a cultural exchange rather than paid employment. Discuss the arrangement's legal status openly with any host studio.
- **Language:** Even basic Japanese ability — or a Japanese-speaking contact — dramatically improves your success rate.

## 🇮🇩 Bali (Indonesia)

Bali is paradise for a working artist — warm, cheap, creative, and full of international clients. But Indonesia has some of the strictest foreign work rules in Asia, and they actually enforce them.

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

Indonesia runs a **special task force specifically targeting illegal foreign workers**. This is not a drill. Bali's enforcement is active.

**Formal legal pathways:**

- **C7C Visa (Practical Skill Demonstration):** This is the most realistic option for a short guest spot. It's a short-term single-entry artist visa for professionals demonstrating skills live. Stay: **30 days, non-extendable**. Cost: ~**IDR 3,000,000 (~$185 USD)**. Processing: 5–10 business days. Requirements: an employment contract in Indonesian signed by both parties, plus the host studio's company registration documents. The host studio must be a legally registered company.
- **Artist KITAS E23R:** A 6-month work visa for longer engagements. Multiple entry. Cost: ~**IDR 22,950,000 (~$1,400 USD)** including government work levy (~$600) and KITAS fees. Requirements: certified diploma translation, 5 years of documented work experience, Bali address, and a qualified sponsoring company. Processing: 1–2 months.
- **Full KITAS/IMTA (employer-sponsored):** The standard long-term route. Requires a properly registered company with minimum IDR 1 billion capital. Cost to artist: ~$1,500 USD. Processing: up to 3 months. You must be outside Indonesia when applying.

**The guest-spot reality:** The C7C visa is the most practical route, but your host studio needs to be a fully registered business. Many smaller shops and beach-front operations don't have the legal structure to sponsor one. Artists doing informal paid work on tourist visas risk **deportation and fines**.

**Visa-on-arrival (30 days, extendable once to 60 days total):** Fine for attending the Bali Tattoo Expo as a non-working visitor. Not sufficient for receiving payment.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

- No specific prohibition on tattoo machines, needles, or inks as personal/professional items.
- **Duty-free allowance:** Personal goods under **USD 500** in total value are exempt from customs duty. Equipment above that may attract scrutiny.
- **Quantities matter:** Goods in commercial quantities (multiple identical items) may be treated as business imports and taxed. Bring working quantities, not wholesale stock.
- **Needles:** No specific ban. Medical-grade packaged needles in reasonable quantities clear without issue.
- **Inks:** No known import restriction. Declare if carrying significant amounts.
- **Strict prohibitions (completely separate):** Narcotics carry the **death penalty** in Indonesia. I shouldn't need to say this, but I've seen enough to say it anyway.

### 🎨 Cultural Sensitivities

- **Hindu culture:** Bali is a Hindu island within a majority-Muslim country. Respect for ceremonies, temples, and sacred sites is non-negotiable.
- **Temples:** Wear a sarong and sash when entering temple grounds (usually available to borrow). Do not enter temples during menstruation (signs are posted). Do not climb on sacred structures.
- **Religious symbols:** Avoid tattooing designs that mock or appropriate Hindu deities without appropriate knowledge and intent. Balinese tattoo tradition incorporates protective and spiritual symbolism — respect it.
- **Tattoo culture on Bali** is generally very positive. The island has a long-standing tattoo tradition and the scene is welcoming to foreign artists.
- **Rest of Indonesia:** More conservative. Bali is an exception within a Muslim-majority country. If you travel to Java or other islands, different norms apply.

### 🤝 Finding Guest Spots

- **Instagram** is the primary platform. DM Canggu and Seminyak studios with your portfolio. The Bali tattoo community is very Instagram-driven.
- **Bali Tattoo Expo:** Attend or apply as a booth artist. The expo is the best networking opportunity on the island.
- **In-person networking:** Bali's tattoo community is tight-knit. Showing up, visiting studios, and buying local artists a coffee goes a long way.
- **C7C visa timing:** If you want to do it properly, start the visa process with your host studio at least 3–4 weeks before your planned arrival. The studio needs time to prepare the paperwork.
- **Best months:** April–October is dry season and peak tourist period. More clients, more energy. November–March is wet season — quieter but still workable.

## 🇸🇬 Singapore

Singapore is small, expensive, orderly, and absolutely brilliant. The tattoo scene punches way above its weight for a city-state. But the compliance culture here is real — Singapore takes rules seriously, and so should you.

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

Singapore's Ministry of Manpower does not have a streamlined pathway for short-term visiting tattoo artists. Here's what exists:

- **Employment Pass (EP):** For foreign professionals earning at least **SGD 5,000/month** (minimum, higher for some sectors). Requires employer sponsorship. Processing: ~3 weeks. Not practical for a short guest spot.
- **S Pass:** For mid-skilled workers earning at least SGD 3,150/month. Requires employer sponsorship and a quota system.
- **Work Pass Exempt (WPE) Framework:** Allows certain short-term activities without a formal work pass, but tattoo artistry at a private studio is not clearly within the exempt categories.
- **Work Permit (Performing Artiste):** This scheme — which allowed short-term work for performing artists — **has been discontinued as of June 1, 2026**, due to widespread abuse.

**Practical reality:** Some artists work informal guest spots on a Tourist/Social Visit Pass (most Western nationalities get 30–90 days visa-free). This is technically non-compliant. **Singapore's enforcement is stricter than Thailand or Bali.** For legitimate arrangements, work with the host studio to explore whether they can apply for an EP or establish a formal contractual consulting arrangement. Legal advice from a Singapore immigration lawyer is recommended for extended stays.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

- **All goods imported into Singapore are subject to GST and/or duty.** However, personal professional equipment (your working kit) is generally admitted as personal effects without customs duty, provided quantities are personal-use only.
- **Needles:** Singapore Customs has noted that tattoo equipment (including needles) may be subject to control requirements. For personal quantities arriving as a traveller's professional tools, declaration is advisable.
- **Inks:** Declare large quantities. Singapore has strict chemical safety compliance.
- **Bottom line:** Carry professional documentation. Declare at customs if in doubt. Singapore is extremely orderly about compliance — don't try to be clever.

**Daily budget for a working artist:**
- Budget: SGD 80–150/day (~$60–110 USD) — hostels, hawker centre meals, public transport.
- Mid-range: SGD 150–250/day (~$110–185 USD).
- **Hawker centres** are your best friend: meals from SGD 3–8. Every neighbourhood has at least one. Some of the best food in the world for the price.
- Singapore is one of Asia's most expensive cities. Budget accordingly.

### 🎨 Cultural Sensitivities

- **Tattoos in Singapore:** Generally accepted in urban culture. Cosmopolitan city with significant Western influence; tattoos carry little social stigma.
- **Conservative corporate culture:** Heavily tattooed professionals may cover up in formal client-facing roles, but this doesn't affect visiting artists.
- **Public behaviour:** Singapore has strict public conduct laws (no littering, no chewing gum). No direct tattoo relevance, but it reflects the compliance culture.
- **Multicultural sensitivity:** Singapore's diversity (Chinese, Malay, Indian, expatriate communities) means awareness of cultural symbols in tattoo designs is valued. Avoid designs that may be offensive to any of the three main religious communities.
- **Religious sites:** Muslim mosques, Hindu temples, and Buddhist temples are present. Cover arms and legs when entering.

### 🤝 Finding Guest Spots

- **Instagram:** Dominant platform. DM studios directly.
- **Singapore Ink Show:** Apply to exhibit — it's the highest-profile event and a great way to meet local studios. [sginkshow.com](https://www.sginkshow.com)
- **Singapore Tattoo Convention:** Second major event, equally good for networking.
- **Studios known for hosting international guests:** Hansan Studio (Korean artists), Wild Paw Tattoo Co., Kiku Space, Tooth & Nail.
- **Timeline:** Approach studios at least **3 months ahead**. Singapore studios often have long client wait lists and need advance notice.
- **Work permit caveat:** Be transparent with any studio about the arrangement. Singapore's compliance culture means reputable studios are more likely to insist on exploring legal arrangements.

## 🇭🇰 Hong Kong

Hong Kong is compact, electric, and has a tattoo scene that's both sophisticated and deeply rooted. It's also got one of the most interesting new visa schemes in Asia for visiting artists.

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

Hong Kong has a separate immigration system from mainland China.

- **General Employment Policy (GEP) Visa:** The primary route for skilled foreign workers. Requires a confirmed job offer, employer sponsorship, proof the role can't be filled locally. Processing: ~4 weeks. Not practical for a short guest spot.
- **Short-Term Visitor (STV) Scheme:** Expanded November 1, 2025 to **17 sectors**, with 490 authorised host organisations. Allows eligible foreign professionals to work for **up to 14 consecutive days per visit without an employment visa**, provided they're invited by an authorised organisation. The **Arts and Cultural sector is one of the 17 designated sectors**. Over 1,520 non-local arts/culture talent had entered under this scheme by mid-2025.
  - **What this means for you:** If a Hong Kong tattoo convention or arts organisation authorised under the STV scheme invites you, you can legally participate and receive payment for up to 14 days. This is the cleanest short-term pathway for convention participation.
- **Working Holiday Visa:** Available to citizens of participating countries (Australia, NZ, Canada, etc.) aged 18–30.
- **Tourist entry:** Most short guest spots happen informally on tourist entry. Enforcement at the individual level is rare.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

Hong Kong is a free port with **essentially zero tariffs on most goods**. This is the easiest country in the guide for bringing in equipment.

- No specific restrictions on tattoo machines, needles, or inks.
- Pack professionally. Declare large quantities of commercial goods.
- Hong Kong is generally relaxed about personal professional equipment.

## 🇰🇷 South Korea

South Korea is in the middle of the biggest legal shift in the global tattoo industry right now. After 33 years of tattooing being classified as a medical procedure, the country has finally legalised it. The scene is exploding, and the opportunities for guest artists are growing fast.

### ✈️ Visa & Work Permit Rules

**The legal revolution:** For 33 years (since a 1992 Supreme Court ruling), tattooing in South Korea was classified as a medical procedure. Only licensed medical doctors could legally tattoo. Artists faced up to 5 years in prison and fines of up to KRW 50 million (~$36,000). The law was under-enforced against Korean artists but presented significant risk for foreigners.

**The Tattooist Act (September 2025):** South Korea's National Assembly passed the Tattooist Act on September 25, 2025, with a unanimous **195–0 vote**. The law legalises tattooing by non-medical personnel through a new government licensing system requiring annual hygiene education. It takes effect after a two-year grace period from promulgation by President Lee Jae Myung.

**Current status (April 2026):** The law is in its transition period. Korean artists are operating more openly than ever. But for foreign artists, the visa question is separate from the legalisation issue.

**Visa options:**

- **C-4 Short-Term Employment Visa:** For paid temporary work under 90 days. Covers performers, consultants, creative professionals — tattoo artistry may qualify. Requires a sponsoring Korean company to provide an employment/performance contract. Processing: 5–14 business days at Korean embassy. **Cannot be obtained inside Korea** — apply from your home country.
- **E-6 Cultural Arts Visa:** Longer-term (1+ year) for artists and performers. More extensive vetting.
- **Tourist / K-ETA entry:** Most Western passport holders enter visa-free for 90 days. Doing paid work on tourist entry remains illegal, even with the new Tattooist Act.

**Equipment customs caution:** Some customs officers during the transition period may question intentions when seeing tattoo equipment. A studio or convention invitation letter is highly advisable.

### 🧳 Bringing Your Equipment

- Given the historical legal ambiguity, bringing visible tattoo equipment to Korea was previously risky. Under the Tattooist Act transition, this is easing — but caution is still warranted.
- **Needles:** No specific import ban, but customs may interpret tattoo needles as indicating intent to work illegally (on a tourist visa). Carry invitation documentation.
- **Practical advice from the community:** Use plain unlabelled cases. Keep things discreet in packaging. Carry documentation: invitation letters, proof of convention participation, professional portfolio.
- **Power:** Korea uses 220V, Type F (European) plugs. Bring appropriate adapters if you're using non-European equipment.

### 🎨 Cultural Sensitivities

- **Rapidly changing environment:** The Tattooist Act reflects a major cultural shift. K-pop celebrities openly displaying tattoos has significantly reduced stigma, especially among under-35s.
- **Remaining stigma:** Broadcasting still censors tattoos. Older generations and conservative communities associate tattoos with antisocial behaviour.
- **Jjimjilbang (public baths):** Many traditional Korean baths and saunas prohibit tattoos, though this is increasingly relaxed in urban/tourist-facing facilities. Look for tattoo-tolerant spots or book private rooms.
- **Military context:** Korea has mandatory military service. Many tattooed Korean men conceal tattoos during service. Tattoos remain controversial in traditional and military-adjacent settings.
- **Religious imagery:** Korea has significant Christian, Buddhist, and Confucian communities. Be thoughtful with religious symbols in designs.

## 🎒 Universal Packing Guide for Tattoo Artists Flying in Asia

After years of packing, repacking, and learning from mistakes at customs desks across the continent, here's my definitive packing approach.

### Equipment Checklist

**Carry-on (always):**
- [ ] Tattoo machine(s) in shockproof, foam-padded hard case
- [ ] Power supply and foot pedal (if wired)
- [ ] Clip cords / RCA cables
- [ ] Small selection of cartridges/needles in original sterile packaging (checked is also fine)
- [ ] Grips and tips
- [ ] Practice skin (useful for demonstrations at customs)
- [ ] Stencil supplies (thermal paper, stencil solution)
- [ ] Phone/tablet with portfolio loaded
- [ ] All lithium battery devices (wireless machines **must** be carry-on — international aviation rule)

**Checked luggage:**
- [ ] Ink bottles (double-bagged to prevent pressure leaks)
- [ ] Additional needles/cartridges in original packaging
- [ ] Disposable supplies (gloves, barriers, cups, paper towels)
- [ ] Larger stencil printer if bringing one
- [ ] Aftercare supplies for clients
- [ ] Power adapters (Type A for Japan, Type F for South Korea, Type G for Singapore/Hong Kong, Type B/C for Thailand/Bali)

### Documentation to Carry (printed + digital copies)

1. **Convention invitation letter or studio invitation letter** — establishes legitimate professional purpose. This is the single most important document you can carry.
2. **Professional business cards and portfolio** (printed booklet or loaded on tablet).
3. **Sterilisation records / certificates** for needles if available.
4. **Work permit / visa documentation** if applicable.
5. **Translations** of key documents into the destination country's language (especially useful for Japan and Korea).
6. **Insurance documentation** — travel and professional liability.
7. **Proof of return flight** — immigration officers in several countries ask for this.

### Tips for Customs Across All Six Countries

- **Organize everything.** A clean, clearly labelled professional kit inspires confidence at customs. A messy bag of loose needles inspires questions.
- **Know your story.** "I'm a professional tattoo artist attending [convention name / visiting studio name] in [city]." Have the invitation letter ready.
- **Declare if in doubt.** In Singapore and South Korea particularly, proactive declaration is better than being caught trying to sneak equipment through.
- **Keep quantities reasonable.** Working quantities of supplies are fine. Wholesale quantities look like commercial import and attract duty.
- **Label everything** with your name and profession.
- **Consider shipping ahead** for extended trips. DHL or FedEx to the host studio avoids airline liquid restrictions and reduces carry-on weight. Best for multi-week trips.
- **Buy locally** where possible. Major tattoo supply brands (Cheyenne, Bishop, Kwadron, Black Claw) are available in Bangkok, Seoul, and major cities. Buying needles and ink locally eliminates customs concerns entirely.

**Perplexity** is brilliant for real-time research. Before every trip, I run searches like:

- "Best tattoo studios in [city] that accept guest artists 2026"
- "Tattoo convention [city] 2026 dates and booth application"
- "Current visa requirements for [nationality] working in [country] 2026"
- "Customs rules for importing tattoo equipment into [country]"
- "Cost of living in [city] for a solo traveller per day"

Perplexity pulls from current sources and cites everything, so you can verify. I use it to check for rule changes right before I fly — visa rules can shift between when you plan a trip and when you actually go.

### Translate Client Consultations

**Claude** is excellent for translation and client communication. I use it constantly in countries where English isn't the first language.

**Ready-to-use prompts for travelling artists:**

**Pre-trip planning:**
> "I'm a professional tattoo artist from [country] planning a 2-week guest spot in [city]. Summarise the current visa requirements, typical daily costs, and any cultural considerations I should know about tattooing there."

**Client consultation translation:**
> "Translate the following tattoo consultation notes into [Japanese/Korean/Thai/Indonesian]. Keep the tone professional but friendly. Include aftercare instructions: [paste your standard aftercare text]"

**Design discussion:**
> "I need to discuss a tattoo design with a client who speaks [language]. Translate these questions: 1) What style are you looking for? 2) Do you have reference images? 3) Where on the body would you like it? 4) What size are you thinking? 5) Do you have any allergies to ink or latex?"

**Cultural check:**
> "I've been asked to tattoo [describe design] on a client in [country]. Are there any cultural sensitivities I should be aware of with this imagery in this specific cultural context?"

**Customs preparation:**
> "I'm flying into [airport] with professional tattoo equipment (machines, needles, inks). What should I know about customs procedures? What documentation should I have ready?"

**Studio outreach:**
> "Write a professional Instagram DM in [language] introducing myself as a guest tattoo artist. I specialise in [style]. I'd like to work at their studio for [dates]. Include a polite request to discuss terms and logistics."

These tools won't replace your own research and local contacts, but they'll make you significantly more prepared — especially when you're navigating a new country for the first time.
